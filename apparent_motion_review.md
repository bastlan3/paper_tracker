## Abstract

Apparent motion — the compelling perception of movement elicited by two or more discrete, stationary stimuli presented in rapid succession — stands as one of the most productive phenomena in the history of experimental psychology. This review examines the classical experimental literature on apparent motion, from Exner's earliest quantitative observations in the nineteenth century through the systematic parametric investigations of the Gestalt era to the emergence of dual-process frameworks in the 1970s. Special emphasis is placed on Korte's three laws (1915), which established the first quantitative account of how spatial distance, temporal interval, and stimulus intensity jointly determine the quality of apparent motion. We trace the development of the field through six thematic areas: (1) the discovery and phenomenological taxonomy of apparent motion phenomena by Wertheimer and his contemporaries; (2) the systematic parametric psychophysics codified by Korte and extended by Neuhaus; (3) the role of figural factors — shape, size, spatial frequency, and cognitive context — in shaping motion quality; (4) the phenomenon of phenomenal identity, including the Ternus display and the tunnel effect; (5) the discovery of figural transformation and the geodesic principle during apparent motion; and (6) the emergence of dual-process models distinguishing short-range from long-range motion mechanisms. We identify consensus findings, persistent controversies, and methodological limitations of the classical approach, and conclude that Korte's laws, though descriptively formulated, encode a genuine constraint on spatiotemporal integration in the visual system whose neural implementation remained beyond the scope of the classical programme but has since been substantially elucidated.

**Keywords**: apparent motion; Korte's laws; phi phenomenon; stroboscopic motion; spatiotemporal integration; phenomenal identity; short-range process; Gestalt psychology

## 1. Introduction

The visual system confronts a fundamental challenge: the physical world contains objects that move continuously through space, but the neural signals encoding those movements are discrete, spatially sampled, and temporally bandlimited. Under natural conditions, a moving object traces a smooth trajectory across the photoreceptor mosaic, generating an unambiguous and graded motion signal. Under laboratory conditions — and in the perceptual experience of cinema, television, and electric signage — sequential, stationary images presented at appropriate temporal intervals produce an equally compelling percept of movement, even though no physical displacement has occurred. This phenomenon, known interchangeably as apparent motion, stroboscopic motion, or the phi phenomenon, has been the object of sustained experimental investigation for more than a century.

The scientific importance of apparent motion rests on three intersecting foundations. First, because apparent motion can be precisely induced by two-frame stimuli under fully controlled laboratory conditions, it affords exceptional leverage for dissecting the stimulus conditions under which the visual system constructs motion representations. By independently varying the spatial, temporal, and photometric parameters of the display, investigators can systematically probe the boundaries and internal structure of the motion-computing mechanism. Second, the parametric richness of the phenomenon — its complex dependence on distance, time, luminance, contrast, form, and surrounding context — makes it an ideal tool for mapping the architecture of motion-sensitive mechanisms in the visual system, at both behavioural and neural levels. Third, the vivid experience of motion in the complete absence of any physically moving object constitutes a profound challenge to atomistic or elementarist theories of perception, in which experience is held to be a simple additive function of the energies impinging on individual receptors. Apparent motion demonstrates that the visual system actively constructs percepts through processes of spatial and temporal integration that operate over distances and intervals far exceeding the grain of individual receptor signals.

Among the empirical regularities that emerged from the classical experimental programme, Korte's laws¹ occupy a position of particular prominence. Formulated through systematic psychophysical experimentation by Adolph Korte in 1915, they specify the trade-offs between spatial distance, stimulus onset asynchrony (SOA), and stimulus intensity that together define the conditions for "optimal" apparent motion — the subjectively intermediate perceptual quality that lies between the extremes of simultaneity (both stimuli appear together) and succession (the stimuli appear sequentially with no motion). While subsequent work has nuanced, reframed, and occasionally challenged these laws, they remain the canonical quantitative summary of the spatiotemporal boundary conditions for apparent motion perception, cited in introductory textbooks and advanced reviews alike.

This review covers the classical experimental literature on apparent motion, with particular emphasis on experiments in the Gestalt tradition and their direct successors through approximately 1980. We examine in sequence the founding experimental observations, the systematic parametric programme, contributions from the study of figural and cognitive factors, the phenomenon of phenomenal identity, and the emergence of dual-process models. Throughout, we critically assess methodological issues and identify the questions that the classical programme could, and could not, resolve.

## 2. Background: The Stroboscopic Paradigm and Preliminary Investigations

### 2.1 Exner and the Earliest Quantitative Observations

The history of apparent motion research predates the Gestalt movement by several decades. Sigmund Exner¹ provided the earliest quantitative measurements of stroboscopic motion perception in a monograph on the psychophysics of simple nervous processes, published in 1875. Using pairs of brief electrical sparks delivered to adjacent skin sites, Exner demonstrated that two temporally offset tactile stimuli — separated by approximately 45 ms — produced a clear sensation of motion from one site to the other. While Exner's primary theoretical interest was in the temporal resolution of the nervous system, his demonstration that a motion percept could arise from two spatially separated, temporally sequential events — without any physical motion occurring — established the basic conceptual paradigm that subsequent researchers would refine.

The broader phenomenon of stroboscopic motion was, of course, already known in practice through the Phenakistoscope (Plateau, 1832) and the Zoetrope, mechanical devices that produced the illusion of continuous movement from a sequence of static images presented in rapid succession. These devices exploited the same principle as laboratory stroboscopic studies but at a temporal granularity well-suited to the depiction of natural motion cycles. The perceptual phenomenon underlying cinema was thus empirically familiar — even commercially exploited — before its investigation as a fundamental property of the visual system.

### 2.2 A Phenomenological Taxonomy of Apparent Motion

By the early twentieth century, Gestalt researchers had distinguished several qualitatively different forms of apparent motion, differing in the nature and vividness of the perceived movement. The principal varieties are:

- **Beta motion (β)**: The standard form of apparent motion, in which an object appears to move smoothly and compellingly from one location to another. The phenomenal trajectory of the object through the intervening space is experienced as clearly defined and object-like.
- **Phi motion (φ)**: A "purer" form of movement in which no distinct moving object is perceived. At certain parameter combinations, observers report a vivid impression of movement — a directional flux or displacement — without being able to specify the shape or identity of a moving object.
- **Alpha motion (α)**: Apparent changes in the size of a single stimulus associated with its onset or offset, related to the expanding or contracting of perceived stimulus magnitude.
- **Gamma motion (γ)**: An expansion or contraction percept at the onset or cessation of a single briefly presented stimulus, often described as an object "jumping out at" the observer.
- **Delta motion (δ)**: Apparent motion perceived in the direction opposite to the temporal order of stimulus presentation, observed when the second stimulus is substantially more intense or larger than the first.²

This taxonomy, while not exhaustively systematic, reflects the phenomenological richness of apparent motion and the difficulty of arriving at a unified mechanistic explanation for its many varieties. Korte's laws were formulated specifically with reference to beta motion, and this specificity is important to bear in mind when assessing their generality.

### 2.3 Physical Parameters of Apparent Motion

The phenomenal quality of apparent motion — whether vivid or degraded, whether it approaches the "pure" phi or the object-like beta, whether it degrades into simultaneity or succession — depends on a constellation of physical and contextual parameters:

1. **Spatial distance** (d) between the centres of the two stimulus positions
2. **Stimulus onset asynchrony** (SOA): the time from the onset of the first stimulus to the onset of the second
3. **Interstimulus interval** (ISI): the blank interval between the offset of the first and the onset of the second (ISI = SOA − duration of first stimulus)
4. **Stimulus intensity or luminance** (L): the photometric output of the stimuli
5. **Stimulus duration** (t): the exposure time of each stimulus
6. **Spatial extent, shape, and form** of the stimuli
7. **Retinal eccentricity** and background luminance / adaptation state

Classical researchers characterised the quality of apparent motion along a qualitative phenomenological dimension from "simultaneity" (no motion, both stimuli appear present together) through an intermediate "optimal motion" zone (vivid phi or beta) to "succession" (stimuli appear one after another with no motion). The central aim of the parametric programme initiated by Korte was to map the region of parameter space that defined this optimal zone.

## 3. Gestalt Origins: Wertheimer and the Discovery of the Phi Phenomenon

### 3.1 The Founding Experiments of 1912

Max Wertheimer's monograph "Experimentelle Studien über das Sehen von Bewegung" (Experimental studies on the perception of movement), published in 1912³, represents the founding document of both the systematic experimental study of apparent motion and of Gestalt psychology. In its classic account, Wertheimer's initial observation occurred during a train journey, where the rhythmic alternation of lights suggested a perceptual problem of deep theoretical significance. Returning to the laboratory, Wertheimer collaborated with Wolfgang Köhler and Kurt Koffka — the latter two serving as principal observers — and conducted a series of carefully controlled tachistoscopic experiments.

The experimental design was, in essence, deceptively simple. A first stimulus (typically a bright line or bar, here denoted A), inclined at one orientation, was presented briefly on a screen; after a precisely controlled temporal interval, a second stimulus (B), at a different position and orientation, was presented. By varying the SOA across a range from near-simultaneous to clearly successive, Wertheimer mapped the full phenomenological continuum.

At very brief SOAs (below approximately 30 ms under standard conditions), the two stimuli appeared to co-occur — both were visible simultaneously, with no impression of motion. At somewhat longer SOAs, a vivid impression of apparent motion emerged: stimulus A appeared to move smoothly to the position of B. Wertheimer termed this **beta (β) motion** — the clear perception of an object in transit. At a critical range of SOAs (approximately 60 ms under his standard conditions, though this varied substantially with distance and luminance), the motion underwent a qualitative transformation: observers reported the impression of movement without any clearly defined moving object. No line was seen to travel; instead, a kind of disembodied "motion" or flux was experienced, as if a disturbance propagated from A to B without carrying any identifiable thing. This Wertheimer termed **phi (φ) motion** — pure, objectless movement. At longer SOAs (typically above 200–300 ms, depending on conditions), all sense of motion disappeared and the stimuli were perceived as simple successional events.

### 3.2 Theoretical Implications for Gestalt Psychology

Wertheimer's interpretation of these results was explicitly anti-associationist and anti-elementarist, and it formed the experimental cornerstone of Gestalt psychology. The phi phenomenon demonstrated, he argued, that perceptual experience cannot be decomposed into independent, additive responses to local, punctate stimuli. No analysis of stimulus A or stimulus B, examined in isolation, would predict or explain the emergent percept of motion between them. Motion was a genuine phenomenal property of the *perceptual whole* — it belonged to the relation between the two stimuli as a unitary object of experience, not to either stimulus separately.

This argument attacked the prevailing "bundle hypothesis" — the view that percepts are sums of elementary sensations — at its root. If a motion percept could arise from two stationary stimuli, then motion experience was not a record of retinal displacements but a construction of the visual system, an emergent relational property generated by the system as a whole. Wertheimer's metaphor was evocative: the two neural responses to A and B were "short-circuited," generating a novel phenomenal quality that neither response alone could produce.

This conceptual move had lasting consequences for motion research. It established that apparent motion was not an error or artefact of perceptual processing but a genuine achievement of the visual system — a window into the constructive, integrative operations by which the system builds coherent representations of a dynamic world from discrete sensory signals.

### 3.3 Contemporary Gestalt Investigations

Wertheimer's foundational study rapidly stimulated a cluster of related experiments. Kenkel², working in the same theoretical tradition, investigated what happened when the two successive stimuli were not identical but differed in size, luminous area, or form. His key finding — the phenomenon of **delta motion** — was that when a small and a large stimulus were presented in succession at appropriate intervals, the perceived apparent motion was directed towards the larger of the two stimuli, even when this was in the temporal direction of the second stimulus rather than always the "forward" direction expected from simple temporal priority. Delta motion implied that the perceptual system was not merely detecting spatiotemporal contiguity but was computing a kind of "object correspondence" that took into account the comparative properties of the two stimuli — their sizes and their relative luminances — in determining the direction and character of the perceived motion.

Higginson⁴ explicitly framed apparent motion within the Gestalt theoretical vocabulary, arguing that the phi phenomenon was a prime illustration of the principle of *Prägnanz* — the Gestalt tendency toward the simplest, most regular, most "pregnant" organisation consistent with the available stimulus information. On this account, apparent motion was the "best" percept available to the visual system given the spatiotemporal configuration of the two stimuli, in the same way that a slightly irregular polygon is seen as a regular one, or an almost-closed circle as a closed circle. The system resolves the ambiguity of two successive flashes in favour of the most coherent, motion-consistent interpretation.

This interpretive framework was influential but also somewhat circular: "best" or most "pregnant" was never formally defined in a way that yielded precise quantitative predictions. It was left to Korte to provide the parametric specification that Gestalt principles alone could not deliver.

## 4. Korte's Laws: A Systematic Psychophysics of Apparent Motion

### 4.1 Korte's Experimental Programme

Adolph Korte's 1915 monograph, "Kinematoskopische Untersuchungen" (Cinematoscopic investigations)⁵, constitutes the pivotal empirical contribution of the classical period. Where Wertheimer's work was primarily a demonstration and a theoretical argument, Korte's was a systematic parametric investigation aimed at quantifying the conditions for optimal apparent motion. Korte worked within the research programme Wertheimer had established, using stroboscopic presentation of simple luminous stimuli — typically bright spots or short bars presented against a dark background in a darkened room.

Korte's central methodological innovation was to vary the three principal parameters — spatial distance (d), temporal interval (SOA), and stimulus intensity (L) — independently and systematically, while instructing trained observers to report whether the motion quality was optimal, suboptimal in the direction of simultaneity, or suboptimal in the direction of succession. By triangulating these judgments across a grid of parameter combinations, Korte could identify the locus of optimal apparent motion as a multivariate function and characterise its structure.

### 4.2 The Three Laws

The results of Korte's investigations were condensed into three empirical generalisations that have come to be known as **Korte's laws**:

**Korte's First Law** (distance–time relationship): For optimal apparent motion, as the spatial distance between the two stimuli increases, the temporal interval (SOA) must also increase proportionally. Conversely, for short separations, shorter SOAs produce optimal motion.

**Korte's Second Law** (distance–intensity relationship): For optimal apparent motion, as the spatial distance between the stimuli increases, the intensity (luminance) of the stimuli must decrease. Greater separations require dimmer stimuli for optimal motion quality.

**Korte's Third Law** (time–intensity relationship): For optimal apparent motion, as the temporal interval (SOA) increases, the intensity of the stimuli must also decrease. Longer SOAs require dimmer stimuli.

Together, the three laws describe a surface in three-dimensional distance–time–intensity parameter space on which the optimal motion percept is located. The laws embody a set of compensatory relationships: any one of the three parameters can, within limits, be traded against either of the other two while maintaining the optimal motion condition. A separation that is too large for optimal motion at a given SOA can be brought back into the optimal zone by increasing the SOA or decreasing the luminance, and vice versa.

### 4.3 Phenomenological Quality as Dependent Variable

It is essential to note the phenomenological character of Korte's dependent variable. The "optimal motion" that his laws describe is not a detection threshold in the signal-detection-theory sense but a qualitative judgment along the simultaneity–motion–succession continuum. Observers — all highly trained psychophysical observers schooled in the introspective methods of the Gestalt tradition — were asked to attend carefully to the phenomenal quality of their experience and to report its location on this continuum. The criterion for "optimal" motion was explicitly experiential: the most vivid, most compelling, most "pure" impression of motion, the quality described by Wertheimer as phi.

This phenomenological criterion has several implications. On the one hand, it was sensitive to subtle qualitative nuances of the motion percept that a simple detection or direction-identification measure might miss. On the other hand, it was potentially vulnerable to observer bias, criterion drift, and the known difficulty of communicating and comparing introspective reports across observers, sessions, and laboratories. These methodological concerns would be raised explicitly by Smith⁹ in 1948 and would become increasingly salient as signal detection theory matured in the 1950s and 1960s.

### 4.4 The "Optimal Motion Zone" in Parameter Space

An important consequence of the three-law structure is that optimal motion does not occur everywhere in distance–time–intensity space but occupies a circumscribed, bounded region — the "optimal motion zone." Outside this region, the percept degrades either toward simultaneity (when SOA is too short or distance too small for the given luminance) or toward succession (when SOA is too long or distance too large). The zone has boundaries in all three parameter dimensions, implying that there are minimum and maximum values of each parameter beyond which optimal motion cannot be sustained regardless of how the other parameters are adjusted.

Korte found that these boundaries were not perfectly sharp — the transition from optimal motion to simultaneity or succession was gradual rather than abrupt, consistent with the view that a range of parameter combinations could produce motion percepts of varying quality. The laws describe the *ridge* of this gradient — the set of parameter combinations producing the highest-quality motion — not a binary threshold.

### 4.5 Theoretical Interpretations

Korte himself offered no explicit mechanistic model, remaining at the level of phenomenological description framed within the Gestalt vocabulary. The laws were understood as specifying the conditions under which the visual system could achieve a particular kind of perceptual organisation — the "best" or most "pregnant" motion interpretation of the two-flash sequence — without specifying the neural or computational operations by which this organisation was achieved.

Several more mechanistic interpretations were implicit in the literature of the period and would be developed by later investigators. One natural interpretation is in terms of the temporal decay of neural activation: the first stimulus activates a region of the visual system, and this activation decays over time. For apparent motion to be perceived, the neural trace of the first stimulus must still be active when the representation of the second stimulus arises, and the two must be coupled by the motion-computing mechanism. The first law then reflects the temporal extent of this neural trace: activations produced by stimuli at greater spatial distances must persist longer for the motion signal to bridge the gap, requiring a longer SOA. The second and third laws reflect the dependence of the trace strength and its time course on stimulus luminance: brighter stimuli produce stronger, faster-decaying activations, which couple differently with subsequent stimuli than the weaker, more slowly decaying activations of dimmer stimuli.

This neural trace interpretation, while plausible, remained untested in the classical period: it would require knowledge of the spatiotemporal properties of direction-selective neurons in visual cortex that was not available until the work of Hubel and Wiesel in the early 1960s and the neurophysiological dissection of motion-selective areas thereafter.

### 4.6 The Validity and Scope of Korte's Laws

The qualitative validity of Korte's three laws has been confirmed across many subsequent experiments and a range of stimulus configurations. The direction of each trade-off has proved robust: increasing distance requires increasing SOA (first law), increasing distance requires decreasing luminance (second law), and increasing SOA requires decreasing luminance (third law). These directional relationships hold across a considerable range of stimulus sizes, shapes, retinal eccentricities, and background luminances.

At the same time, important qualifications have emerged. The laws are formulated for isolated, simple stimuli viewed foveally under photopic conditions. When stimuli are complex, when the visual scene contains distractors or surround motion, or when non-standard observers are used, quantitative deviations from the laws are observed. The laws describe the *average* parameter relationships for optimal motion in simple conditions; they do not predict the exact parameter values for any specific condition, nor do they account for the large individual differences in apparent motion sensitivity documented by later researchers.

Perhaps the most important qualification concerns the distinction between SOA and ISI — a conceptual refinement that significantly reframes the first and third laws, as discussed in the following section.

## 5. Parametric Investigations: Extending and Refining Korte's Laws

### 5.1 Neuhaus (1930): Comprehensive Parametric Mapping

Neuhaus's 1930 monograph⁶ represents the most extensive parametric investigation of apparent motion in the classical period. Neuhaus systematically mapped the optimal motion zone in distance–time–luminance parameter space using a substantially wider range of values than Korte had employed, and with more rigorous photometric control of stimulus intensity. His study confirmed the qualitative correctness of Korte's three laws over this extended parameter range while also revealing important structure in the boundaries of the optimal motion zone.

Neuhaus's principal quantitative finding was that the first law — the distance–SOA trade-off — was approximately linear over a considerable range of distances but showed curvature at extreme values. At very large separations, increasing the SOA could not compensate indefinitely: there was a maximum spatial distance beyond which no combination of SOA and luminance produced convincing apparent motion. This "spatial ceiling" implied that the coupling mechanism underlying apparent motion had a finite spatial range — a conclusion that would be powerfully reinforced by Braddick's experiments four decades later.

Neuhaus also examined the luminance-dependent laws (the second and third) with greater rigour than Korte, finding that the relationship between optimal SOA and luminance was not purely monotonic but depended on the absolute luminance level. At very low absolute luminances — below some threshold level — apparent motion quality was uniformly poor at all SOA values, reflecting a minimum activation level required for any motion percept, independent of temporal parameters. This finding suggested that the luminance effects in Korte's second and third laws were not simply a matter of contrast per se but involved the absolute energy of the stimulus in relation to the detection threshold of the underlying mechanism.

An additional methodological contribution of Neuhaus's study was his systematic use of observers with different levels of training. He found that highly trained observers reported more stable and reproducible phenomenal qualities than naive observers, and that the precise location of the optimal motion zone was somewhat observer-dependent — evidence that the phenomenological criterion of "optimal motion" was not invariant across observers but was shaped by experience and, potentially, by response criterion differences. This finding prefigured the methodological critique that Smith would articulate in 1948.

### 5.2 Kahneman (1967) and the Onset-Onset Law

Nearly four decades after Korte, Daniel Kahneman⁷ revisited the question of which temporal parameter was the fundamental determinant of apparent motion quality. In a careful series of experiments, Kahneman independently manipulated the SOA and the ISI by varying the duration of the first stimulus while holding one parameter constant. His central result was unambiguous: apparent motion quality was governed by the SOA — the time from the onset of the first stimulus to the onset of the second — and not by the ISI, the blank interval between the offset of the first and the onset of the second.

When the first stimulus was presented for a long duration, the ISI was correspondingly shortened for a constant SOA, and motion quality remained constant. Conversely, holding the ISI constant while varying stimulus duration (and thus the SOA) produced systematic changes in motion quality, with longer SOAs producing degraded motion (toward succession) and shorter SOAs producing motion of higher quality or none at all (toward simultaneity), consistent with the first law.

This "onset-onset law" has important theoretical implications that bear directly on the interpretation of Korte's laws. It implies that the neural event that initiates the motion-computing process is stimulus *onset* — the appearance of the stimulus — rather than stimulus *offset*, and that the relevant temporal parameter is the delay between the two onset signals, not the gap between them. This is consistent with a model in which apparent motion arises from the interaction between onset-detecting neural mechanisms, whose responses are triggered at stimulus appearance and decay thereafter. The critical temporal variable in Korte's first and third laws should therefore be the SOA, not the ISI — a distinction that is small in the typical conditions of classical experiments (where stimulus durations were brief) but significant for longer-duration stimuli and for mechanistic interpretations.

### 5.3 Kahneman and Wolman (1970): Duration Effects

Kahneman and Wolman⁸ extended the analysis of temporal parameters by examining the joint effects of stimulus duration, ISI, and SOA on apparent motion quality across a wider parameter range. Their results confirmed the primacy of SOA while also revealing that, at very short stimulus durations, the onset-onset law began to break down — an indication that a minimum stimulus duration was required for the onset signal to achieve adequate strength to drive the motion mechanism. This finding was consistent with the photometric saturation effects described by Neuhaus: below a certain energy level (duration × luminance), the onset response was too weak to initiate motion computation regardless of temporal parameters.

Kahneman and Wolman's work thus clarified the structure of the temporal parameter space for apparent motion, distinguishing between the primary role of SOA (governing the temporal coupling between onset responses) and the secondary role of stimulus duration (governing the strength of each onset response). These two dimensions had been conflated in Korte's original formulation, which did not hold stimulus duration constant across conditions.

### 5.4 Smith (1948) and Methodological Critique

Smith's⁹ 1948 study raised programmatic methodological concerns about the measurement of apparent motion thresholds that would prove prescient for the subsequent development of the field. Smith argued that the phenomenological criteria used by earlier investigators — "optimal motion," "simultaneity," "succession" — were poorly operationalised and potentially confounded by observer criterion differences and introspective unreliability. Two observers trained in different laboratory traditions, or the same observer on different occasions, might place the boundary between "optimal motion" and "near-optimal motion" at different parameter values, not because their perceptual sensitivities differed but because their introspective criteria did.

Smith proposed more objective behavioural measures — including forced-choice direction-identification and detection tasks — as necessary complements to phenomenological judgments. Such measures, he argued, could separate perceptual sensitivity from response criterion in a principled way. While Smith's critique did not immediately transform methodology in the apparent motion field, it anticipated the wholesale adoption of signal detection frameworks in psychophysics in the 1950s and 1960s and identified a genuine limitation of the classical parametric programme: the quantitative precision of Korte's laws rested, ultimately, on observer judgments whose reliability and objectivity had not been rigorously established.

## 6. Figural Factors: Shape, Form, and Perceptual Organisation in Apparent Motion

### 6.1 Kenkel (1913) and Delta Motion: The Role of Stimulus Size

The earliest investigation of figural factors in apparent motion was Kenkel's² study of delta motion, already introduced above. Delta motion — the perception of movement directed toward the larger or more intense of two stimuli, opposite to the temporal order of presentation — established the important principle that the direction and character of apparent motion were not determined solely by the spatiotemporal parameters captured in Korte's laws but could be overridden or modulated by the comparative properties of the stimulus objects themselves.

Kenkel's interpretation was Gestalt-theoretical: the perceptual system organised the two stimuli into the "best" available percept, where "best" reflected some principle of minimum change or good continuation applied not just to position but to object properties. A small stimulus followed by a large one at the same location could be interpreted as an object growing in size (gamma-like expansion), or the small stimulus at one location could be perceived as moving toward the large one at another location as if approaching it. The visual system appeared to favour the latter interpretation when the spatial and temporal parameters were appropriate, producing delta motion.

The existence of delta motion demonstrated that Korte's laws were not sufficient to characterise apparent motion in the general case: they described the parametric dependencies for the simplest class of stimuli (identical, isolated spots or bars) but could not account for the perceptual effects of systematic differences between the two stimulus objects.

### 6.2 Kolers and Pomerantz (1971): Figural Change and Shape Transformation

One of the most striking demonstrations of the role of figural factors in apparent motion was provided by Kolers and Pomerantz¹⁰ in a study that directly examined what happened to the shape of the perceived moving object when two geometrically dissimilar stimuli were presented stroboscopically. Using pairs of simple geometric forms — rectangles, ellipses, triangles, and irregular polygons — they showed that when stimulus A (at position 1) and stimulus B (at position 2) had different shapes, the stroboscopically induced apparent motion was accompanied by systematic shape transformation during the perceived transit.

Observers reported that the object they saw moving from position 1 to position 2 was not simply a copy of stimulus A, nor of stimulus B, but appeared to "morph" continuously from the shape of A to the shape of B over the course of its apparent trajectory. If A was a rectangle and B was a circle, the perceived moving object underwent a gradual deformation from rectangular to circular as it crossed the intervening space.

This "figural change" or shape transformation during apparent motion had several significant theoretical implications. First, it demonstrated that the apparent motion system was not merely interpolating a rigid, featureless object between two fixed positions, as a simple position-tracking account would predict. Instead, the system generated a dynamic representation that integrated the form properties of both stimulus objects across the temporal interval, constructing a trajectory that was simultaneously spatial and shape-based. Second, the shape transformation result established that apparent motion computations were not independent of form-recognition and shape-representation processes — the motion mechanism had access to the forms of both stimuli and computed a "path" in form space as well as in physical space.

Kolers⁹ elaborated these results in his 1972 book *Aspects of Motion Perception*¹¹, arguing that apparent motion was not a perceptual failure or low-level illusion but a sophisticated constructive achievement — a form of spatiotemporal inference in which the visual system computed the most internally consistent interpretation of a sequence of form and position changes. The moving percept was, on this view, a hypothesis about the underlying physical event, constrained by both spatial proximity (Korte's first law) and figural consistency (the shape transformation result). This constructivist view of apparent motion, while not elaborated in formal computational terms, anticipated the probabilistic and Bayesian accounts of motion perception that would emerge decades later.

### 6.3 Foster (1971): Spatial Frequency Specificity

Foster's¹² 1971 study addressed whether the apparent motion mechanism operated at a level that was sensitive to the fine spatial structure of the stimuli — specifically, their spatial frequency content. Using sinusoidal luminance grating patches of varying spatial frequencies as the two stimuli in an apparent motion display, Foster examined how spatial frequency (mis)match between the two stimuli affected motion quality.

The key finding was that apparent motion quality was selectively dependent on the match between the spatial frequencies of the two stimulus gratings: displays in which both gratings had the same spatial frequency produced stronger, more compelling apparent motion than displays in which the two gratings differed in spatial frequency, even when all other parameters (distance, SOA, luminance) were identical. The specificity was not absolute — apparent motion of some quality was observed even for gratings of different spatial frequencies — but the preference for matched spatial frequencies was robust.

This result had significant implications for understanding the neural substrate of apparent motion. If the apparent motion mechanism operated at a level of abstraction above spatial frequency — for example, at the level of position codes or object representations — then spatial frequency mismatches should not impair motion quality, since two stimuli in the same position differ only in their spatial structure, not their spatial address. The demonstration that spatial frequency *does* matter implied instead that the mechanism underlying apparent motion is intimately coupled with the spatial frequency-tuned channels that were, in the early 1970s, being intensively characterised in human spatial frequency psychophysics. This suggested that apparent motion, like pattern detection, was mediated by a set of channels, each tuned to a range of spatial frequencies, and that motion was computed within channels, not across them.

Foster's result thus established a link between the classical Gestalt phenomenology of apparent motion and the spatial frequency channel framework that was transforming human vision research in the same period — a link that would be substantially elaborated in subsequent decades.

### 6.4 Navon (1976): The Irrelevance of Figural Identity

An important qualification of the role of figural factors was provided by Navon¹³ in a study of apparent motion under conditions of *directional ambiguity*. In most classical apparent motion studies, a single stimulus at one position is followed by a single stimulus at another position, and the direction of apparent motion is unambiguous. Navon created displays in which motion direction was ambiguous: for example, two stimuli at positions A and B in frame 1 followed by two stimuli at positions C and D in frame 2, where multiple geometrically distinct correspondence solutions were possible. The question was whether the *identity* (shape or form) of the stimuli influenced which correspondence solution — and hence which direction of motion — was perceived.

Navon's finding was clear: figural identity was largely irrelevant for resolving directional ambiguity under standard viewing conditions. When one solution corresponded to short spatial distances between matched stimuli and another to longer distances between matched stimuli, observers consistently selected the short-distance solution. When the short-distance solution involved a shape mismatch between corresponding stimuli and the long-distance solution involved a shape match, observers still predominantly favoured the short-distance (proximity) solution. The shape of the stimuli exerted little influence on which motion direction was seen.

This result provided a striking qualification of the conclusions one might draw from Kolers and Pomerantz: while figural transformation certainly occurs as a *consequence* of apparent motion (once correspondences have been established), the *establishment* of correspondences — the solution of the "correspondence problem" of determining which stimulus in frame 1 goes with which in frame 2 — is governed primarily by spatial proximity, in direct conformity with Korte's first law. Object identity, shape, and form are, in this sense, downstream of spatial proximity in the apparent motion computation.

### 6.5 Sigman and Rock (1974): Perceptual Intelligence

A counterpoint to the primacy of spatial proximity was advanced by Sigman and Rock¹⁴, who demonstrated that perceptual inference based on implicit knowledge of object properties and physical regularities could, in certain circumstances, override the spatial proximity heuristic. In their key demonstrations, stimulus configurations were designed so that the "proximity" solution — the one that Korte's first law would favour — corresponded to a physically or object-wise unlikely event (for example, an object instantaneously appearing at a discontinuous location, defying physical continuity), while the "long-distance" solution corresponded to a physically plausible motion (for example, rotation of a rigid object through a quarter-turn).

In such conditions, a substantial proportion of observers favoured the physically coherent, cognitively plausible interpretation — the long-distance solution — over the spatially proximal but physically implausible one. Sigman and Rock described this as evidence for **perceptual intelligence**: the capacity of the perceptual system to bring to bear knowledge of the physical and object world in resolving motion ambiguities. The system was not, they argued, a mere proximity minimiser; it was a probabilistic reasoner that weighted the prior plausibility of different motion interpretations against the sensory evidence.

While the "perceptual intelligence" framework was not formalised in quantitative terms by Sigman and Rock, it captured an important aspect of apparent motion that purely stimulus-based accounts like Korte's laws missed: the sensitivity of the percept to the broader environmental and object-level context. This contextual sensitivity would be elaborated in subsequent years in both computational models and in studies of object-level and scene-level influences on motion correspondence.

### 6.6 Shepard and Judd (1976): The Geodesic Principle

A landmark demonstration of higher-level constraints on apparent motion was provided by Shepard and Judd¹⁵, who presented observers with two views of a three-dimensional object (typically a complex wire-frame or solid figure) at different orientations in depth, using two-frame stroboscopic presentation. Despite the two-dimensionality of the projected stimuli, observers reliably and vividly perceived the object rotating through three-dimensional space — and the perceived axis of rotation corresponded, to a close approximation, to the axis that produced the *shortest* rotation arc between the two views, i.e., the *geodesic* in the mathematical space of rigid rotations (SO(3)).

This "geodesic principle" was theoretically significant on several counts. First, it demonstrated that apparent motion computations could operate over three-dimensional representations, not merely over two-dimensional retinal images — the visual system was computing trajectories in depth-structured space. Second, the geodesic character of the perceived trajectory implied that the apparent motion mechanism implemented something close to a *minimum-change* or *minimum-energy* principle: among all possible rotation paths between the two views, the one that minimised the total rotation angle was the one that was perceptually preferred.

In relation to Korte's laws, the geodesic principle can be understood as a high-dimensional generalisation of the spatial proximity rule. Korte's first law — that shorter spatial distances favour apparent motion — is, in two-dimensional space, equivalent to a principle of minimum translational distance. Shepard and Judd's result extended this minimum-distance principle to the domain of three-dimensional rotational motion, suggesting that the underlying computational preference was for the *simplest* or *most economical* transformation, and that this preference was not confined to translational motion in the fronto-parallel plane.

Shepard interpreted this in the context of his broader programme on the "internalization of physical regularities": the idea that the perceptual system had acquired, through evolution or learning, implicit knowledge of the geometrical constraints governing the motion of rigid objects in three-dimensional space. For apparent motion, this implied that the system's solution to the spatiotemporal correspondence problem reflected not just proximity in position space (Korte's first law) but proximity in the larger space of physically plausible object transformations.

## 7. Phenomenal Identity and Object Persistence

### 7.1 The Problem of Phenomenal Identity

A fundamental question underlying apparent motion research — one that was articulated explicitly by Gestalt theorists but whose significance pervades the entire classical literature — is the question of **phenomenal identity**: when a stimulus at position A is followed, after a brief interval, by a stimulus at position B, does the observer experience *the same object* at a new location, or two different objects in succession? Without phenomenal identity — the sense that the entity at A is the numerically same individual as the entity at B — there can be no motion percept, only a sequence of appearances.

The importance of this question lies in its implication that apparent motion is not merely a matter of detecting spatiotemporal contiguity or motion signals but involves a higher-level process of object individuation and persistence: the visual system must construct a representation of an individual object that persists and moves across time, not simply detect a motion signal in the local retinal image. This is a fundamentally different kind of process from simple low-level motion detection.

Metzger¹⁶ addressed the conditions for phenomenal identity systematically, finding that it was related to but not identical with apparent motion quality: in some conditions, observers could experience a vivid percept of motion without a clear sense that the endpoint object was the *same individual* as the starting-point object, while in others, a strong sense of identity could persist even when the motion quality was poor. Metzger proposed that phenomenal identity and motion quality were dissociable dimensions of the stroboscopic percept, with somewhat different parametric dependencies — a conclusion that would be substantially elaborated by Ternus.

### 7.2 The Ternus Display: Element Motion and Group Motion

The most enduring contribution to the phenomenology of apparent motion and phenomenal identity in the classical period was Ternus's¹⁷ 1926 study, which introduced the paradigm now universally known as the **Ternus display**. In its canonical form, the Ternus display consists of three collinear dots:

- **Frame 1**: dots at positions 1, 2, and 3
- **Frame 2**: dots at positions 2, 3, and 4

Two dots (at positions 2 and 3) are common to both frames; one dot (position 1) is unique to the first frame, and one (position 4) to the second. At appropriate SOAs, this display produces one of two qualitatively distinct and mutually exclusive motion percepts:

1. **Element motion**: The two dots at positions 2 and 3, being common to both frames, appear phenomenally stationary. The dot at position 1, appearing only in frame 1, appears to jump to position 4, which is occupied in frame 2. This percept involves short-range, element-by-element correspondence: each element at a new location in frame 2 is matched to the spatially nearest element in frame 1.

2. **Group motion**: The three dots appear to move as a coherent group, shifting one position to the right. All three dots in frame 1 are mapped to all three dots in frame 2 as a collective unit, requiring a long-range, group-level correspondence in which spatial proximity of individual elements is overridden by the global coherence of the group.

Ternus found that the SOA (or ISI) was the principal parameter governing which percept was seen: at short ISIs (with the blank interval between frames being minimal or absent), element motion predominated; at longer ISIs, group motion was more frequently reported. This ISI dependence of the type of apparent motion seen had several important implications.

First, it demonstrated that temporal parameters influenced not merely *whether* apparent motion was perceived (as in Korte's laws) but *what kind* of motion — the level of perceptual organisation at which correspondences were computed. The temporal interval did not simply regulate a single scalar quantity (motion quality) but switched the system between qualitatively different correspondence strategies. Second, the Ternus result implied the existence of at least two motion-computing processes with different temporal sensitivities: one that established short-range, element-by-element correspondences at short ISIs, and one that established long-range, group-level correspondences at longer ISIs. This was an early empirical hint of the dual-process architecture that would be explicitly articulated by Braddick and others in the 1970s. Third, the Ternus display provided a paradigm case for phenomenal identity: element motion preserved the identity of positions-2-and-3 dots across frames (they "stayed put"), while group motion involved a collective displacement of all three, illustrating that phenomenal identity could be assigned at both the individual element level and the group level.

### 7.3 The Tunnel Effect and Amodal Completion in Motion

The "tunnel effect," investigated systematically by Burke¹⁸, addressed the maintenance of phenomenal identity under conditions of spatial occlusion. Burke's paradigm involved an object moving toward and then disappearing behind an opaque occluder (the "tunnel entrance") and reappearing on the other side of the occluder (the "tunnel exit"). Despite never being visible during the occlusion interval — the object was completely hidden by the occluder throughout its passage through the "tunnel" — observers reliably reported perceiving a *single object* that had passed through the tunnel and emerged at the other side. Phenomenal identity was maintained across a spatiotemporal gap filled by physical occlusion.

Several features of the tunnel effect are theoretically significant. First, it demonstrated that the visual system could maintain object identity across intervals of invisibility that were substantially longer than the maximal SOA for apparent motion between spatially separated flashes. The presence of the occluder — a contextual cue signalling that the gap in visibility was attributable to occlusion rather than to object disappearance — dramatically extended the temporal range over which identity persistence operated. Second, the tunnel effect thus demonstrated an interaction between apparent motion mechanisms and scene-level parsing: the same spatiotemporal gap in object visibility was processed differently depending on whether it was attributed to occlusion or to genuine object disappearance.

Burke's result anticipated much later work on amodal completion, object permanence, and the "object file" framework (in which objects are represented by persistent tokens that accumulate and retain their properties across perceptual interruptions), and it established that phenomenal identity in apparent motion was not a simple function of spatiotemporal parameters (as Korte's laws might suggest) but was modulated by the higher-level interpretation of the scene.

### 7.4 Duncker (1929): Induced Motion and Contextual Effects

Karl Duncker's¹⁹ 1929 study of **induced motion** addressed a related set of questions about the role of spatial context in motion perception. In Duncker's paradigm, a physically stationary target object was surrounded by a larger moving frame. Observers perceived the target as moving in the direction opposite to the frame's motion — they attributed the relative motion between frame and target to the target rather than (or in addition to) the frame, even though the frame was the only physically moving element. This "induced motion" was robust and compelling, persisting even when observers knew the physical facts of the display.

For the apparent motion literature, induced motion is relevant because it established two principles that bear directly on the interpretation of Korte's laws. First, motion perception is contextually sensitive: the motion or non-motion of one object influences the perceived motion of another, even when the two objects are physically independent. Second, the visual system does not compute motion in a purely egocentric or absolute reference frame but in a system of relative references, assigning motion to figure elements relative to ground elements. These contextual effects were not captured by Korte's laws, which were derived from conditions using isolated stimuli on neutral, structureless backgrounds.

Duncker's work also reinforced the Gestalt principle that figure-ground organisation was relevant to motion perception: whether a given element was seen as "figure" or "ground" determined whether it was assigned motion or interpreted as a stationary reference frame. This assignment was not determined by motion signals alone but by the broader perceptual organisation of the scene. The same physical motion could be attributed to different objects depending on the figure-ground relationship — a striking illustration of the constructive, context-dependent nature of motion representation.

## 8. Short-Range and Long-Range Processes: A Dual-Process Framework

### 8.1 Kolers (1963) and the Relationship Between Real and Apparent Motion

A fundamental question that ran through the classical apparent motion literature was whether apparent and real physical motion were processed by the same or different neural mechanisms. An early direct investigation was provided by Kolers²⁰ in 1963, who systematically compared the perceptual characteristics of real (continuous) motion with those of stroboscopic apparent motion across a range of conditions. Kolers found several systematic differences between the two types of motion: for example, the threshold for accurately identifying the direction of real motion was lower than that for apparent motion; adaptation effects produced by prolonged exposure to real motion (such as the motion aftereffect, or "waterfall illusion") were not fully reproduced by equivalent exposure to apparent motion under all conditions; and the phenomenal quality of apparent motion was more variable across stimulus parameters than that of real motion.

These differences implied that apparent and real motion were not fully identical at the neural level, even when they produced similar phenomenological experiences under optimal conditions. Kolers interpreted the data as evidence for multiple motion mechanisms, capable of supporting the motion percept under different stimulus conditions and with partially distinct properties. This multiple-mechanism view prefigured the dual-process framework that would be explicitly articulated a decade later.

Kolers¹¹ developed this perspective at length in his 1972 book, *Aspects of Motion Perception*, which synthesised the available psychophysical evidence on the relationship between real and apparent motion, the role of figural factors (as in the Kolers and Pomerantz¹⁰ shape transformation results), and the parametric structure documented by Korte and Neuhaus. Written at a time when the neurophysiology of direction-selective neurons in visual cortex was beginning to emerge from the work of Hubel and Wiesel (1962–1968) and of Barlow and Levick, Kolers's book was one of the first serious attempts to connect the classical psychophysical data on apparent motion with the emerging neurobiology of motion processing. While Kolers was appropriately cautious about premature neural identification of psychophysical mechanisms, the book established a theoretical framework in which apparent motion was understood as a multi-level constructive process involving both low-level, stimulus-driven components and higher-level, cognitively modulated ones.

### 8.2 Braddick (1974): The Short-Range Process and D-max

The most influential investigation of the limits of the motion-computing mechanism in the classical period was Braddick's²¹ 1974 study, which introduced the random dot cinematogram as a tool for isolating a low-level, pre-attentive motion-detection process. Building on the random dot stereogram technique developed by Julesz²⁶ for investigating depth perception, Braddick constructed two-frame displays in which both frames consisted of random patterns of dots. In the second frame, every dot was displaced by a fixed amount *d* in a fixed direction, so that the entire pattern shifted. Because the dot patterns were otherwise identical and spatially uncorrelated, no individual dot could be identified or "tracked" across frames — there were no unambiguous feature correspondences. The only information available to signal the direction of displacement was the global statistical regularity of the displacement field.

Braddick measured the largest displacement *d* for which coherent directional motion could be perceived in such displays — a parameter he termed **D-max**. His finding was clear: D-max was approximately 15 arcminutes of visual angle under standard conditions of foveal viewing at photopic luminance levels. Beyond this displacement, no coherent motion was perceived, even though the physical displacement was clearly defined by the experimenter. The dot pattern of the second frame appeared spatially uncorrelated with that of the first, as if the two frames were independent random dot patterns.

The existence of D-max established several important conclusions. First, there was a motion-computing process with a finite and relatively small spatial range — approximately 15 arcminutes — that operated on the local spatial structure of the luminance pattern, without requiring identification of individual features or objects. This was a genuinely low-level, spatiotemporal correlation process. Second, this "short-range process," as Braddick termed it, was distinct from the "long-range" apparent motion described in Korte's classical experiments, which routinely employed stimulus separations many times larger than D-max. Long-range apparent motion, on this account, required either the identification of specific features or objects in each frame and their correspondence across frames — a fundamentally different, higher-level process.

Third, D-max depended on stimulus spatial frequency content and retinal eccentricity in ways that were consistent with the short-range process being implemented by spatially frequency-tuned, retinotopically organised neurons — the kind of mechanism associated with the simple and complex cells of primary visual cortex. This supported Foster's¹² earlier finding of spatial frequency specificity in apparent motion and provided a more precise neural localisation of the short-range mechanism.

### 8.3 Pantle and Picciano (1976): Psychophysical Dissociation of Two Motion Systems

Independent psychophysical evidence for two distinct motion systems was provided by Pantle and Picciano²² in a study using adaptation and selective suppression of motion percepts. Pantle and Picciano designed a "multistable motion display" in which multiple motion interpretations were simultaneously possible — a stimulus in which the response of two different motion mechanisms produced competing percepts. By selectively adapting one mechanism through prolonged exposure to a particular stimulus type, they could selectively reduce the contribution of that mechanism while leaving the other relatively intact.

Their key result was a double dissociation: adapting to one motion type selectively reduced the percept associated with one motion system, leaving the other intact, and vice versa. This double dissociation provided strong evidence that the two perceptual modes were not merely different output states of a single mechanism but reflected the operation of two genuinely distinct, independently fatigable processing systems. One system — with properties resembling Braddick's short-range process — was spatially frequency-tuned and operated over small displacements; the other — the long-range system — was less spatially frequency-specific, operated over larger displacements, and was sensitive to the identity and features of the stimulus objects.

Pantle and Picciano's study thus provided a psychophysical counterpart to Braddick's stimulus-range manipulation, converging on the same dual-process conclusion through a different methodological approach. The convergence of the two results substantially strengthened the case for two distinct motion mechanisms, each with its own stimulus range, spatial frequency sensitivity, and adaptation properties.

### 8.4 Anstis (1970): Phi Motion as a Subtractive Process

Anstis²³ proposed a mechanistic account of phi motion as an opponent or **subtractive process**, based on a series of demonstrations using stimuli of opposite polarity. His central observation was that phi motion could be elicited by presenting a bright (luminance-increment) stimulus at position A followed by a dark (luminance-decrement) stimulus at position B: observers perceived motion from A to B, but the perceived moving object had the character of a "dark hole" or negative object moving through space, rather than a bright object. The direction of motion was the same as for standard (bright–bright) phi, but the phenomenal quality of the moving entity was inverted in polarity.

Anstis proposed that this result could be explained by a model in which phi motion arose from the neural subtraction of the visual response at A from the visual response at B, after an appropriate temporal delay. When a bright stimulus at A is followed by a bright stimulus at B, the difference signal is positive at B and negative at A — a signal consistent with rightward motion. When a bright stimulus at A is followed by a dark stimulus at B, the difference signal at B is negative (the dark stimulus represents a below-baseline response) — consistent with motion of a "dark" object from A to B.

This opponent-process account elegantly unified several features of phi motion: its occurrence at intermediate SOAs (when the first stimulus's neural trace still contributes to the difference signal); its dependence on stimulus intensity (stronger stimuli produce larger difference signals); and its qualitative character under polarity reversal. It also connected the psychophysics of phi motion to the opponent-process architecture known from colour vision and from the biology of direction-selective neurons, which compute something closely analogous to the spatiotemporal difference described by Anstis.

### 8.5 Braddick (1980) and Anstis (1980): Consolidation of the Dual-Process Framework

Two major review articles published in the same special issue of the *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* in 1980 synthesised the accumulated evidence and established the dual-process framework as the dominant theoretical paradigm for apparent motion at the end of the classical period.

Braddick²⁴ reviewed the full range of evidence for the short-range process, including his own D-max measurements, the spatial frequency specificity of short-range motion, the monocular nature of the process (which failed to cross ocular dominance columns under appropriate conditions), and its independence from object features and cognitive strategies. He proposed a functional specification of the two processes:

The **short-range process** was characterised as a low-level, stimulus-driven, retinotopically organised spatiotemporal correlator with the following properties: (a) a spatial operating range limited to D-max (~15 arcmin under standard conditions); (b) strong spatial-frequency tuning, with the process operating within spatial-frequency channels; (c) monocular processing, without cross-ocular integration; (d) insensitivity to stimulus features beyond luminance, spatial frequency, and orientation; (e) inability to be voluntarily controlled or to benefit from cognitive knowledge of the stimulus.

The **long-range process** was characterised as operating over spatial displacements much larger than D-max, without spatial-frequency specificity, with sensitivity to stimulus colour, shape, and identity, capable of cross-ocular matching, and subject to cognitive and attentional modulation. The long-range process was thus a "higher-level" mechanism that required the identification of specific stimulus tokens or features and their correspondence matching across frames.

Anstis²⁵ provided a complementary review focusing on the phenomenological richness of the apparent motion domain, emphasising the roles of colour, form, texture, spatial frequency, and contextual factors in modulating motion quality. Anstis systematically reviewed the conditions under which chromatic differences alone could support apparent motion, the contributions of spatial frequency to motion quality, and the evidence for perceptual learning and set in apparent motion judgments. While endorsing the short-range/long-range distinction, Anstis argued that neither process was fully homogeneous: the long-range process in particular appeared to be heterogeneous, encompassing both relatively automatic pattern-matching and highly deliberate object tracking at different levels of the visual hierarchy.

Together, the Braddick and Anstis reviews closed the classical period by establishing a dual-process framework that was simultaneously descriptively adequate (accounting for the wide range of phenomena documented since Wertheimer) and theoretically generative (providing a framework within which subsequent neural and computational investigations could be organised). The classical psychophysical data had provided constraints on the architecture of the motion-processing system that neural models would need to satisfy — constraints that, in subsequent decades, proved highly productive.

## 9. Discussion

### 9.1 The Empirical Legacy of Korte's Laws

Looking across the full arc of the classical literature, the empirical legacy of Korte's three laws is both substantial and appropriately nuanced. The qualitative correctness of the three directional relationships — that distance, SOA, and luminance trade off in determining apparent motion quality, and that each parameter can partially compensate for changes in the others — has been confirmed across many independent experimental programmes, a range of stimulus configurations, and diverse observer populations. The laws have proved durable as a first-order characterisation of the spatiotemporal boundary conditions for apparent motion, providing a common vocabulary and quantitative framework for the field.

At the same time, each law has been refined and qualified by subsequent work in ways that illuminate the underlying mechanisms and the conditions of their application. Kahneman's⁷ onset-onset law reframes the first and third laws in terms of SOA rather than ISI — a refinement that is theoretically significant, as it identifies the triggering event for the motion computation (stimulus onset) rather than the temporal gap between events. Neuhaus's⁶ extended parametric mapping reveals that the optimal motion zone has finite spatial and temporal limits, implying a bounded spatial range for the motion mechanism that would later be operationalised as D-max by Braddick.²¹

The second and third laws — involving luminance — have proved more complex than Korte's original formulation. The dependence of optimal motion on luminance is not a simple monotonic function but interacts with absolute luminance level (below a photometric threshold, motion fails regardless of temporal parameters), with retinal adaptation state, and, as Foster's¹² work implies, with the spatial frequency content of the stimuli. These interactions were not captured by Korte's original formulation, which was derived from conditions of fixed, moderate photopic luminance.

Most importantly, Korte's laws were derived from simple, isolated, uniform stimuli on neutral backgrounds — conditions chosen precisely to minimise confounding figural and contextual factors. The subsequent literature demonstrated comprehensively that apparent motion was profoundly shaped by figural factors (Kenkel,² Kolers and Pomerantz,¹⁰ Foster,¹² Navon¹³), by object-level and cognitive factors (Sigman and Rock,¹⁴ Shepard and Judd¹⁵), by phenomenal identity processes (Ternus,¹⁷ Metzger,¹⁶ Burke¹⁸), and by spatial context (Duncker¹⁹). None of these influences is captured by Korte's laws. They are, in this sense, an incomplete description of apparent motion in the general case — but a highly accurate and productive description of apparent motion in the simplified conditions for which they were designed.

### 9.2 Methodological Limitations of the Classical Programme

The classical research programme rested almost entirely on phenomenological methods: trained observers reported the quality of their perceptual experience in qualitative or semi-quantitative terms, using descriptors such as "optimal motion," "simultaneity," and "succession." This methodology had genuine strengths: phenomenological methods, when practised by disciplined observers, can detect subtle qualitative differences in percepts that more objective, forced-choice methods might obscure, because they do not require the reduction of the percept to a single binary or ordinal judgment. The richness of the phenomenological vocabulary developed by Wertheimer, Korte, and their successors — distinguishing phi from beta, element motion from group motion, delta from gamma — reflected genuine qualitative distinctions in the apparent motion phenomenon that were scientifically productive.

The methodological weaknesses of the phenomenological approach were, however, substantial. As Smith⁹ correctly diagnosed in 1948, the criteria for "optimal motion" were not operationalised in a way that permitted reliable comparison across observers or laboratories. Two observers using nominally the same criterion might be operating at systematically different points on the simultaneity–motion–succession continuum, so that the quantitative values of Korte's laws — the specific SOAs and distances at which optimal motion occurred — might not be reproducible across studies. The literature in fact shows considerable quantitative variability in reported optimal-motion parameters, a variability that plausibly reflects criterion differences at least as much as genuine differences in perceptual sensitivity.

A related limitation was the small number of observers typically employed. Most classical studies used between two and ten observers, often including the investigator. The representativeness of these samples for the broader human population was never established, and the observer-dependence reported by Neuhaus⁶ — that trained observers gave more stable and reproducible results than naive ones — raised the question of whether the reported laws described a general property of human vision or the specific pattern of responses of a small cohort of highly trained, theoretically motivated observers.

These methodological concerns do not invalidate the classical programme — the qualitative phenomena and their parametric dependencies are robust — but they caution against treating the specific quantitative values in Korte's laws as precise neural-level specifications.

### 9.3 The Neural Substrate: What Classical Experiments Could and Could Not Address

The classical apparent motion literature was conducted before the modern era of motion neurophysiology. The discovery of orientation-selective and direction-selective neurons in cat striate cortex (Hubel and Wiesel, 1959–1968), the characterisation of area MT as a motion-dedicated cortical region (Dubner and Zeki, 1971; Zeki, 1974), and the development of spatiotemporal energy models of motion (which lay beyond the temporal scope of this review) all occurred at or after the end of the classical period. Classical researchers could characterise the stimulus conditions for apparent motion with considerable precision, but they could not directly link these conditions to specific neural circuits or computations.

Braddick's²¹ D-max measurement came closest to providing a neural-systems inference: the ~15 arcminute spatial scale of D-max, and its scaling with spatial frequency content and retinal eccentricity, strongly implied a mechanism implemented in the spatial-frequency-tuned, retinotopically organised neurons of early visual cortex — mechanisms whose properties were being characterised in parallel by spatial frequency psychophysicists and visual neurophysiologists. But this inference was indirect, based on the convergent properties of known low-level neurons rather than on direct measurement.

The dual-process framework of Braddick²⁴ and Anstis²⁵ proved highly prescient, anticipating neural distinctions that subsequent research would operationalise in terms of magnocellular and parvocellular pathways, in terms of the dorsal (action) and ventral (recognition) processing streams, and in terms of the complementary roles of V1/MT for low-level motion extraction and temporal-lobe and parietal-lobe areas for high-level object and scene representation. The classical behavioural constraints thus provided a set of functional specifications — a short-range process with small spatial range, spatial frequency tuning, and monocular organisation; a long-range process with large spatial range, feature sensitivity, and cognitive modifiability — that neural investigations would spend the following decades filling in.

### 9.4 Korte's Laws in the Context of Spatiotemporal Integration

From the perspective of modern computational theories of motion, Korte's laws can be interpreted as specifying the operating range of a family of spatiotemporal correlators. A spatiotemporal correlator — the computational core of both the Hassenstein–Reichardt motion detector (developed for insect motion vision) and the spatiotemporal energy model (developed for primate vision) — computes the product of the visual signal at one spatial location and time with the signal at a spatially offset location and a later time. A detector so constructed responds optimally when two spatially separated signals occur at the temporal delay that corresponds to the detector's preferred speed: the ratio of spatial offset to temporal delay.

In this framework, Korte's first law — that spatial distance and SOA must co-vary for optimal motion — is exactly what would be expected from a bank of velocity-tuned correlators. Each correlator is tuned to a specific velocity (ratio of distance to time), and "optimal motion" is obtained when the velocity implied by the stimulus parameters falls within the tuning range of the available detectors. The first law's description of distance–time covariation describes the iso-velocity contour for the most sensitive detector. The existence of a maximum distance and maximum SOA beyond which no combination of parameters restores optimal motion implies that the bank of velocity detectors is bounded — that there are no detectors with arbitrarily large spatial offsets or arbitrarily long temporal delays.

Korte's second and third laws — involving luminance — are less directly predicted by the simple correlator model and require additional assumptions about gain control or response normalisation. One natural interpretation is that stimulus luminance determines the amplitude and time course of the neural response to stimulus onset, and that the correlator's optimal operation requires the two input signals to have a specific ratio of amplitudes and temporal overlap. High-luminance stimuli produce large, fast-decaying onset responses; optimal correlation requires them to be presented with shorter delays and smaller spatial offsets. Low-luminance stimuli produce smaller, more slowly decaying onset responses; optimal correlation tolerates larger delays and offsets. This amplitude-dependence of the optimal spatiotemporal interval provides a mechanistic account of Korte's second and third laws that is more explicit than Korte's own Gestalt-theoretical framing, even if still approximate.

## 10. Future Directions

The classical programme in apparent motion research, extraordinarily productive as it was, left a number of important questions either unaddressed or only partially resolved, and these became the foci of subsequent investigation.

**Neural substrate and mechanism.** The link between psychophysical parameters and neural implementations remained largely implicit throughout the classical period. Subsequent neuroscience connected psychophysical D-max measurements to the preferred spatial offsets of direction-selective neurons in V1 and MT, and spatiotemporal energy models formalised the computational operations underlying simple motion detection. The development of reverse-correlation and maximum-likelihood estimation methods in the 1980s and 1990s allowed the spatiotemporal tuning of motion mechanisms to be measured with unprecedented precision, providing the mechanistic account of Korte's laws that the classical programme could not.

**Individual differences and developmental trajectory.** Classical studies used small cohorts of highly trained observers, leaving open the question of variability in apparent motion processing across the human population and its developmental trajectory. Subsequent developmental investigations demonstrated that D-max, spatial frequency selectivity, and long-range feature tracking had distinct developmental profiles, with some aspects of short-range motion detection present in very young infants while aspects of long-range processing matured over the first months of postnatal life.

**Colour and chromatic apparent motion.** The classical tradition worked almost exclusively with achromatic stimuli varying in luminance. Whether apparent motion could be driven by pure chromatic differences — stimuli equated in luminance but differing in hue — became an important diagnostic question in the 1980s and 1990s, with implications for the role of the magnocellular (luminance-sensitive) and parvocellular (chromatic) pathways in motion computation.

**Computational formalisation.** The classical data accumulated a rich set of behavioural constraints without a formal computational model that could generate quantitative predictions for novel conditions. The development of probabilistic correspondence models, Bayesian frameworks for motion estimation, and neural network implementations of the short-range and long-range processes provided the theoretical formalisation needed to move beyond empirical law description to genuine prediction and explanation.

**Multisensory and cross-modal apparent motion.** Classical research was entirely restricted to visual apparent motion. The investigation of analogous phenomena in audition (saltation, auditory apparent motion, the kappa effect) and somatosensation, and their interactions with visual motion, opened new research programmes and raised questions about the extent to which apparent motion reflected modality-specific or modality-independent spatiotemporal integration mechanisms.

## 11. Conclusion

The classical experimental literature on apparent motion, from Exner's earliest quantitative measurements through the founding Gestalt experiments of Wertheimer and Korte to the dual-process synthesis of Braddick and Anstis, represents one of the most sustained, coherent, and productive empirical programmes in the history of perceptual psychology. Over a period of seven decades, a remarkably consistent research community — working with limited technology, small samples, and phenomenological methods that would not satisfy modern psychophysical standards — accumulated a body of knowledge that has proved durable, generative, and foundational for subsequent neural and computational investigations.

Korte's three laws occupy a central position in this legacy. Formulated through careful parametric psychophysics of simple stroboscopic stimuli, they provide a quantitative summary of the spatiotemporal boundary conditions for apparent motion that has withstood more than a century of scrutiny. Their directional content — that distance, temporal interval, and luminance trade off in specifying the optimal motion zone — captures a genuine constraint on the spatiotemporal integration performed by the visual system, a constraint that finds its mechanistic explanation in the tuning properties of velocity-selective neural detectors. The qualifications and refinements introduced by subsequent investigators — Neuhaus's extended parametric mapping, Kahneman's onset-onset law, Braddick's D-max — did not overturn Korte's laws but deepened their interpretation, linking them to specific neural mechanisms and computational operations.

Beyond the laws themselves, the classical period established that apparent motion was irreducibly rich, depending not only on the spatiotemporal proximity of the two stimuli but also on their figural properties (shape, size, spatial frequency), on the higher-level correspondence processes that established object identity across frames, on the phenomenal persistence of objects through occlusion and temporal gaps, and on the availability of multiple qualitatively distinct motion mechanisms with partially overlapping but non-identical domains of operation. The enduring insight — present already in Wertheimer's 1912 founding experiments and elaborated through every subsequent decade — is that apparent motion is not a perceptual error, not an artefact of insufficient temporal resolution, not a failure to detect the absence of physical motion. It is, instead, a constructive achievement of the visual system: the successful application, to spatiotemporally discontinuous input, of the same motion-building operations that serve the perception of physically continuous motion in the natural world.
